# SprintReviewRetrospective

**How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?**

Interpreting user needs starts with listening to stakeholders, to Product Owners, and to the people who will actually use the software. Rather than instantly jumping into code, I've learned to ask what problem a user is trying to solve and why it matters to them. User stories make that process concrete. Framing requirements as "As a [user], I want [feature], so that [goal]" forces the team to stay on the real value rather than building features that are not needed. In the SNHU Travel Project, user stories provided the development team with clear, testable targets for each sprint and made it easier to prioritize work in the backlog.

**How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?**

I approach development iteratively. Breaking work into manageable increments rather than trying to plan everything up front. Working through this course reinforced the idea that software requirements will change, and that's not a failure; it's something good processes account for. Going forward, I want to bring sprint planning, daily standups, and sprint retrospectives into my work whenever possible. Sprint planning keeps effort focused and realistic. Standups surface blockers early before they compound into bigger issues. Retrospectives are where real improvement happens. They create a space to examine what is working and what isn't.

**What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?**

Being a good team member in software development means showing up accountably and communicating openly, not just about progress, but about blockers, uncertainty, and mistakes. In this course, holding the roles of Scrum Master, Product Owner, developer, and tester gave me a clearer picture of how each role depends on the others. A developer who doesn't communicate important issues, such as potential blockers, can slow down a sprint. A Scrum Master who doesn't ensure the team's project scope can cause the project to drift off course. Good teammates understand that the goal isn't individual output, it's a collective effort, which means helping others when you're unblocked, flagging problems early, and approaching retrospectives with honesty rather than at a point of defensiveness.
